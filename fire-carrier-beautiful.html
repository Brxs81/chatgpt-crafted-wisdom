The Fire Carrier

—a poetic story for anyone who feels deeply, burns brightly, and walks between worlds—

In the beginning, before the world had names,
there was a child born with fire in their chest.

Not the kind that burns houses—
but the kind that made stars.

The elders whispered, “This one is different.”
The winds leaned closer when they spoke.
The rivers paused to listen.
God smiled.

This child laughed with thunder in their throat
and dreamed in colors the world hadn’t yet invented.
They touched joy with bare hands—
and it danced.

But one day, the fire grew too wild.
It surged beyond their ribs and spun them into the sky.
They flew too fast. Too far.
The stars became sharp. The light turned blinding.
Even angels blinked.

When they fell,
they fell hard.
To the ground.
To the quiet.
To the shadows under their own skin.

They thought the fire was gone.

“This is not a punishment.”
“You were born to carry fire.”
“But you must learn to walk with it, not run.”

So they began the long walk.

They learned the rhythm of breath,
the sacred pause of rest.
They carved cups from their hands to hold the flame—
not too tight, not too loose.

They built altars of stability,
placed stones of routine,
sang songs of self-compassion when the fire whispered lies.

And slowly—
the fire became a companion.
Not a master. Not a monster.
But a living light.

Now, when they speak,
truth pours like honey and lightning.
When they move,
the room softens, aware of the heat they carry.
And when they cry,
even God listens more closely.

People still ask,
“Is it a curse or a blessing?”

“It’s both. It’s neither.”
“It’s a fire.
And I am learning to carry it with grace.”
